# Privacy: The HTTPS Moment for Blockchain
**Zac Williamson** (Aztec Foundation) --- Monroe Stage

Hello. All right, I guess we're on to the next talk. Hello, everybody. Good afternoon. My name is Zach. I am the co-founder of Aztec, a privacy-preserving layer 2 for Ethereum. And I'm here to talk about how privacy Web3 is going to be the HTTPS moment for blockchain and how I think, and how Aztec's work connects to all of this. So privacy technology transformed the internet. I think for those of us who were around in Web 1.0, this is fairly obvious, but maybe for people who are a bit younger, who weren't aware of how the web used to be, this might not be a well-understood story where back in the 1990s, when you have the internet in its first incarnation, Web 1.0, largely static websites, limited bandwidth, very low throughput because everybody was using dial-up connections and remarkably, all internet traffic was fully transparent. You did not have well-established encryption primitives to transmit information privately. More importantly, such encryption algorithms were still kind of considered classified from that Cold War era mindset and were considered kind of munitions, the weapon that couldn't be used by mere civilians. So the way that we use the internet today for e-commerce, for finance, for banking, wasn't possible. You had people like the VP of Visa saying, don't send anything over the internet that you don't want to be read by 40 million people. Does that sound familiar to anybody today working in blockchain? And people could glimpse the future of what the internet would become, but they didn't have the capabilities to realize it. This led to an enormous amount of investment and an enormous amount of attention and then the dot-com crash in 2000 because all these ideas, this grand vision of what the internet was supposed to be, largely failed to materialize at that point in time. And I would like to draw a direct parallel to that and perhaps what we're going through today in blockchain where if we think about those cypherpunk visions and ideals of Bitcoin, the Ethereum white paper, what people wanted Ethereum to be, this decentralized banking institution where you could bank the unbanked and everybody would be transacting on-chain, Bitcoin imagined itself as a payments network where people would transact in Bitcoin, not as a store of value. We're in a similar situation, I believe, and I believe that the two conditions to resolve blockchain's current issues are the same as the conditions that allowed Web2 to succeed. Web2 came about when you had broadband connections and you had high transaction throughput, high information throughput, and privacy by default, HTTPS, where every single request that you're sending and every little response you're getting from a server is end-to-end encrypted. You have a chain of attestations through digital certificates so that you understand where your data is coming from, but you can't be snooped on by anybody. You know your payment information, your sensitive details, they're not visible to observers. It's the power to selectively reveal oneself to the world. We've had the scaling equation down in blockchain for some time, and now privacy is starting to arrive. And I've been working in this space on privacy for eight years, and it's very good to see, finally. But this is going to be completely transformational. We already just heard Paul earlier talk about how institutions are ready and eager to adopt blockchain-based technology once you have privacy technology embedded in it so that you can enable selective disclosure, you can both form KYC, you can hide information from observers and competitors. But it extends far beyond enterprise and institutions. It extends to the people in this room. It extends to the consumer applications that we want to build. It extends to people who want to create financial products and services and applications that compete on a level playing field with Web2 and TradFi. That compete with them and not necessarily are those products. And so how do we fix Web3's privacy problem? Because privacy is a hot narrative, but could I get a show of hands here for how many people regularly use blockchain-based privacy technology? That's four of us. It's just a narrative. The technology has matured in people's eyes in this industry to the point where you can see the future, but you can't grasp it yet. Yet. And I believe this is about to change. I believe it perhaps changed yesterday, and I will explain why. Blockchain needs privacy. You can't build institutions, whether they're companies or decentralized organizations, or something in between. You can't run those completely transparently. You can't do your payroll transparently. You can't have contentious votes transparent and open to manipulation and lobbying. You can't govern in the open. And real-world transactions, the interactions that we have with each other day-to-day, they require information asymmetries, where I know something you don't. You know something I don't. This could be as simple as voting. I know how I voted in an election. You don't know how I voted, but you know my vote counted. Or it could be performing a sanctions check on somebody, where they need to give you a certain amount of information. You have all the other information required to perform KYC compliance checks. You don't want to share it. Maybe you can't share it. And privacy enables information asymmetries. It enables identity access management. It enables information from the real world and from Web2 to be piped into Web3 smart contracts, distributed smart contracts, decentralized autonomous code running on a blockchain. It allows that sensitive information to be placed into those smart contracts, fully encrypted, yet those smart contracts, those autonomous programs, can reason about that data. And this is relatively radical. It allows private payments to scale. It allows high-quality understanding of who somebody is. To give an example, if you look at your phone and you look at the apps on your phone, because we're in a blockchain conference, I imagine people here, many of them, will have blockchain applications. They will have crypto apps, and they will have Web2 apps. And these things are like oil and water. They don't mix. It's very clear if you have a blockchain app or a Web2 app. And this is because crypto applications don't actually know who you are. If I open up my wallet, I am a string of hexadecimal characters. I might be an ENS domain. But what that relates to in the real world is completely unknowable by that application. Whereas if I open up my banking application or if I open up even my social media, that application has a much richer understanding of who I am as a real person. And that allows for more complexity. And the fact that there is no in-system intuitive way of communicating that information into a smart contract without doxxing yourself, without reading information that cannot be revealed, that prevents these crypto apps from becoming infrastructure that's used by mainstream applications. It prevents the boundary between crypto apps and real-world apps from dissolving away. I think we all understand that when crypto succeeds, if we used blockchain technology as infrastructure for things like payment rails or banking services, you won't have crypto apps anymore. You'll just have apps that use blockchains the same way that apps use IPv6, and they don't talk about it because they don't care. It just works. And if we don't have privacy, from the mouth of the Ethereum Foundation itself, all that we're building is just a information panopticon. It is surveillance technology that does the exact opposite of what everybody here wants it to achieve. So this is what I've been doing for the last eight years. I would argue that Aztec built the modern privacy stack, and we built a lot of it from pretty much from scratch, from ideas and mathematics into papers into code into products. First you need an extraordinarily fast zero-knowledge proving system. If you want privacy, that means you're constructing your proofs locally on your client, on your phone, on your laptop, and you need something tailored towards that, that can handle that, which is what we produced. You then need a programming language that actually understands privacy semantics, that understands the difference between public information and hidden information, that understands private state and how to manipulate it and the semantics around private state. And finally, you need an execution environment and a state machine that can execute privacy-preserving transactions, a global distributed ledger. That's the Aztec network. And we've been building this for many years now in various incarnations. And I'm very happy to announce today that Aztec is definitively landed and launched. We launched our alpha version of our network on the Ethereum mainnet yesterday. And yesterday, the first Aztec transaction came through. So this is now the culmination of something that we've been working for for a very long time. You can now write privacy-preserving smart contracts, deploy them to a fully permissionless decentralized network, and execute those transactions. You can bridge into and out of them from Ethereum, and it all works. And it's opening up a new design space on Web3. So this is just a smattering of the applications and projects that are being built on Aztec, from bridges and private decentralized exchanges to on-chain games to stablecoins to compliance portals. It's the start of something very radical and very new that's not been possible before because all these applications in some form or another have much more of an understanding of who the user is than a regular application because that user has private state.
