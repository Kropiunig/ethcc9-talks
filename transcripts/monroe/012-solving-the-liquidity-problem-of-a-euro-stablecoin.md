# Solving the liquidity problem of a euro stablecoin
**Gísli Kristjánsson** (Monerium) --- Monroe Stage

Hello, I'm Kisli, the CEO and co-founder of Monerium. So we've been issuing stablecoins for seven years now, and we have learned a thing or two along the way. Even as a centralized issuer, there's a lot of things that we do off-chain, whether it's managing the reserves, reconciling the issuance against those reserves, or just onboarding and KYCing users. But our true north star has always been DeFi and kind of us playing a role in moving the world's money supply on-chain. I have to admit, in some cases, this has been a test of character. For example, in the very early days, when minting a single euro and transferring cost more than 80 bucks. Ouch, the future finance was obviously here. But I think for us, we are here because we believe that DeFi and blockchains have something important, a characteristic that is the composable nature and the atomic settlement. And that's why we are here. So with having said that, I just want to dive into the talk of solving the liquidity problem of a euro stablecoin issuer. In order for this talk to be productive, I think it's important that we understand the stablecoin market structure. So we already know that stablecoins have reached a payment network scale. Last year, the annual transaction volume exceeded $30 trillion. And in its size, the TVL, the market is already at $300 billion, up 10x from 2020. And last November, US Secretary Scott Bassett said that he predicted that the stablecoin market would be in the $3 trillion range. And the leading financial institutions agree that somewhere in the range of $2 to $4 trillion will be the stablecoin market in the same time range. If we look at the euro, however, it accounts for roughly 0.35% of the market. This obviously means, by definition, that there's not a lot of liquidity in the market for euro stablecoin. And this creates high slippage. On the U.S. side, we have mostly two major issuers accounting for all the liquidity in the market. But on the European continent, we see a different story. The liquidity is fragmented across roughly 20 issuers, creating liquidity silos for the already scarce liquidity. So we see the rails work, but the structure does not. So going from the high level and into something more concrete, let's focus on some of the key challenges that a stablecoin issuer faces. Distribution. How do you get the stablecoin in exchange for fiat currency? And the U.S. stablecoins, they have traditionally been minted and burned through centralized intermediaries. These are the exchanges, on and off ramps, and other third parties. Now, if you are a euro issuer, that becomes more challenging because these exchanges, they don't have the incentive to distribute the tokens because they don't have stablecoin euro pairs on their exchange. Liquidity. Meaning how efficient can you actually trade the asset against a stablecoin? So going in and out at a scale. And we usually measure liquidity in how much slippage or spread there is on the pair. And here again, the European issuer is at a disadvantage because again, the crypto exchanges, they don't necessarily have a European stablecoin pair. And thirdly, the use cases that drive the adoption of the stablecoin beyond just crypto speculation. I think it's evident that for any stablecoin issuer around the world, if they want to be successful, they need to break out and be useful outside of the crypto realm. And for the U.S. dollars, they've already been very valuable to users and citizens in countries around the world where they have weak currency. Unfortunately, the euro has not been seen as a strong currency for this use case, but maybe the political winds are changing in our favor. I've talked extensively in the past at ECC and elsewhere about many of these challenges that stablecoin issuers faces. But I want to double click on one of them. I want to double click on liquidity. So let's break it down. There are essentially two ways for a user to trade a stablecoin. These are centralized exchanges and decentralized exchanges, both with their own advantages and disadvantages. If we take the centralized exchange, a listing fee is usually required. And this can range from anywhere between $100,000 to $500,000 per exchange. And there is a certain liquidity that is needed as a base or else the token will get unlisted. So this is generally where market makers come into the picture. They are placing orders on behalf of the issuer, both on the buy side and the sell side. And they will do so in exchange for a monthly fee, but also in exchange for an inventory of the stablecoins. And this is quite different to many of the other DeFi projects or crypto projects that may actually have had some allocation from the pre-mine that they can actually use to seed the initial liquidity. For a stablecoin issuer, you need to have one-to-one backing with a euro in the bank account, making this very costly for the team behind the stablecoin. The decentralized exchanges usually don't require any listing fee. Anyone can create a pool where you deploy a smart contract, a pool, essentially, and you provide the stablecoin and the asset. And the pool itself will generally capture revenue through fees, trading fees, and they'll share that with the liquidity providers in order to incentivize more liquidity into the pools. However, this liquidity usually isn't, sorry, the revenue from the trading usually isn't enough to incentivize the users and have the required yield for the liquidity providers. So the issuer is expected to provide extra incentives. And this can be seen, this is somewhere in the range, of €100,000 per €1 million of liquidity across different pools and, of course, across different chains, across the different chains. So this effectively creates a position that is, I would say, not very efficient. And because of this exuberant cost for the European stablecoin issuer, the euro has mostly been paired with other stablecoins, such as USDC. And for those pools, the fee revenues are even lower. So I think it's safe to say that the AMM pools that we have today are generally not very efficient for stablecoin swaps. But as I mentioned before, DeFi and decentralized exchanges have certain properties that are both interesting and advantageous. Number one, liquidity can be shared between pools and settled in an atomic fashion. And number two, assets can be traded with an intermediary asset. So even though a pool does not exist between, let's say, asset A and C, a trade can be settled between a pool that has A and B and another pool B and C, effectively creating a route from A to C. This would be the equivalent in TradFi of being able to buy Bitcoin on Kraken and sell the Bitcoin on Coinbase for ETH and being guaranteed that you are able to settle both or none at all. So intent-based trading emerged as a new paradigm for trading DeFi, which was supposed to alleviate one of the biggest hurdles, which was just user experience. So in the intent-based trading world, you have solvers that try to compete for the best routing of the trade and therefore the best liquidity and therefore the best price. The solvers themselves, they will aggregate the liquidity they see in all the pools that exist, but more importantly, they also can source the private liquidity. And they will then, at the end of the day, settle all the trades in one atomic swap across all the different pools. And it's composable. So for a European issuer like Monerium, when we were faced with trying to create this liquidity, we basically had two options. Either we raise funds to seed the pools, pay the market makers, and so on and so forth. Or alternatively, we tried to come up with something clever. And we thought, what if we could effectively use the atomic nature of the blockchain to not only settle two pre-minted assets, but instead inject the liquidity as the trade was being settled? This was a key insight that we had and understanding that we had an unfair advantage over everybody else who wanted to provide liquidity for the euro. So last year, I was here on stage and we were kind of hinting at what we were thinking about. And this year, I'm here to announce that we've cracked the nut. We are launching what we call liquidity on demand. And the way it works is whenever a user goes to an intent-based trading venue, they will be able to see liquidity that does not exist. But when they accept the trade, what happens behind the scenes is the solver will query Monerium that is now not just a stablecoin issuer, but also a market maker for our tokens against USDC. And the solvers themselves, they will be able to and are able to source this liquidity on demand for the trade. So the euro doesn't exist until the trade happens. And we can be guaranteed that we receive the USDC and then we sell it off instantly in order to back up the new supply of the euros. So we are starting with Ethereum mainnet and we are allowing our users to trade up to €100,000 in both directions in the beginning. And we are also going to be adding other chains. And the cool thing about that is when we do, we are actually able to share the liquidity between the chains in contrast to DeFi as it exists today, where you have a lot of idle capacity in one pool, but another pool is being used heavily and there's a need to rebalance between the chains. Of course, by extension of DeFi, we can also create markets, not just for the euro against USDC, but the solvers will find all the paths to all the assets that are in your wallet today, effectively creating a euro market for all the tokens in the wallet.
