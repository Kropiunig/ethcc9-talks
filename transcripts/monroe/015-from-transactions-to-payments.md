Hello, hello, GM Jim. Good morning, everyone. Thanks so much for coming. I know it's early, but I wanted to really come here and open up the vibes and just get everyone excited. And as you can see also from the screen, my name is Pedro Gomes. I'm the founder of Wallet Connect. I've created multiple iterations of what Wallet Connect is today that now connects over 700 wallets. And now we are reaching a new world of payments across merchants, across the world. Crypto has really found its place. When I say it has found its place, it's because there was always this kind of identity crisis where Ethereum tried to be a lot of things like the world computer. It tried to do decentralized governance. It tried to do NFTs. But realistically, when you think about the tokenization of real world assets and even the money markets efficiency and instant settlement across the world, it really is the value proposition that has impacted people's lives. And through infrastructure, that's what we try to also help Ethereum to be even more accessible, to be even easier to use and really build this platform so that every wallet developer could reach their audience. But there was always this like missing piece. Like if you think about the origins of crypto as a whole, Ethereum was an evolution over Bitcoin. But even in the original white paper for Bitcoin, it was always about payments. So why was a Bitcoin white paper written in 2008 and Bitcoin was launched in 2009 and it's still in 2026 and payments is probably one of the most underserved categories in the whole crypto. Yes, we have tokenization. We have trading. We have perps. We have prediction markets. We have everything that we could possibly imagine. New primitives from both DeFi to TradFi. But payments is still not a solved problem. And that's something that we want to have an impact as a Wallet Connect. When you think about payments, it's a little bit different, right? Everything that has been done in the past with crypto was extremely technical and required a lot of user education. Because when you think about transactions, they're not a tool for users. They're not a tool even for merchants. They're a tool for developers to build applications. Essentially to build the programmable money that kind of brought us all here. But then if we really want to solve payments, we can't use transactions as the core primitive for what it is, the experience when you exchange for products and services. It can't be this highly complex technical object that we use as the primitive for this exchange because payments don't work like that. So you could think of payments as just sending funds, but it's so much more than that. In fact, there is an actual process to how to do payments. And it's broken down into three core primitives, the authorization, the clearing, and the settlement. And in fact, many times we say crypto already does payments, but what crypto does is settlement. The finality of funds reaching from A to B. Authorization, on the other hand, is the first step of every payment flow. And when I say every payment flow, we're talking about bank transfers. We're talking about card payments, QR code payments. Every form of payments follows these three steps. And authorization is even the question whether or not am I able to receive these funds. There's a lot of regulations across the world and this will change from jurisdiction to jurisdiction, but the merchant can't just bluntly accept payments. And these laws also change. Like even, for example, in Europe, we until this point, we were able to spend cash up to a certain amount and they even lowered that threshold. So the authorization aspect, even for cash itself, has changed where the merchant can't just accept any sort of amount of cash. While in card payments, it has different thresholds and different rules. But for example, for crypto, one of the most common ones is, is this an OFAC sanctioned address? That's one of the authorizations that needs to be run before receiving the funds. And then in clearing, it has to be about the routing. How do you actually move these funds? Who is actually going to receive the funds? Who is actually the payment provider involved? How is the bank account going to receive the funds on the behalf of the merchant? What fees are going to be participating in this? And there's the whole monitoring and accounting aspect that comes with the payment. And then when all of this is concluded, then you actually do the settlement. Once you actually have finalized the payment, then you actually move the funds. So crypto has to this day only done one third of what it is, the actual payment flow. On top of that, there's two types of payments. These can be just distinguished by push and pull. Essentially, push is what we also are very familiar in crypto, which is you as a consumer or as a sender are the one initiating the transaction. You're the one actually going to the blockchain, putting a payment that actually transfers funds from A to B. But because we haven't run the authorization and clearing, push payments essentially kind of run the process in reverse. You're starting with the settlement first and you haven't done the authorization and clearing. And many times this requires you to have an escrow approach. Yes, escrows are not necessarily bad, but they're not the great experience because you're basically removing the funds from the consumer, putting them temporarily within escrow, making a decision whether or not to receive the funds. And then you might even need to spend gas to refund the funds back if the authorization does not pass. Pull payments, on the other hand, are much simpler. But for the longest time, it was so hard to do pull payments in crypto because delegating power or delegating an authorization for someone else to move funds on your behalf was very complicated. Sure, we had the RC20 that had allowances. This was part of the standard a long time ago, but even allowances required the user to spend gas to just give an authorization for someone else to move funds. Then future standards like permit2 improved this experience with the DAI stablecoin and eventually USDC even had an upgrade on top, which is the 3009, which is transfer with authorization, which is a very commonly well known now because it's actually how card payments work. And this is why pull really has won as a category because it really ensures that all checks and balances are done before the payment is actually authorized. Then you actually reduce the merchant risk because you also don't have this moment where there's an escrow where funds are just sitting there and you don't know if you are even able to accept those funds. And also it gives you a little bit more an instantaneous feeling because we are able to verify an authorization off-chain and then give you the guarantee that we will complete the settlement after without you having to wait for the finality of depositing into escrow, running the checks, and might even have the refund. So actually even from an user experience perspective, it's much faster to do a pull payment than a push payment. And this is thanks to a lot of standards that stablecoins have developed like permit2 and transfer with authorization. On top of this, wallet connect has always been a big proponent that the world is multi-chain and now we also have to think about the multi-token world. A multi-token world becomes a little bit more important when it comes to payments because while prior wallet connect always focused on the wallet-chain relationship, now we have to think about the wallet-chain and token relationship because as we mentioned, we need to focus on what does a pull payment experience look like? Because if a pull payment requires us to check the balance, to check the OFAC sanctions, and actually do these transfer with authorization standards, we also need to support every possible token pattern. And of course, if we need to accept Ethereum tokens, we actually need to use an escrow approach because you can't do a transfer with authorization with a native token. And this is not something that merchants want. Again, the biggest challenge with payments, we're not building for developers. We have to avoid complexity, risk, and user education. They just need to accept funds to pay for products and services. They do not care about how it works. In fact, they wish there was only one currency, one settlement, and one integration. And that's exactly what we're trying to achieve with Wallet Connect Pay. We already launched Wallet Connect Pay. A few wallets already have upgraded their SDKs to support this new flow. And essentially, you could think of Wallet Connect Pay as two things. It is both a backwards compatible universal flow that allows existing wallets to connect their wallets as many years we've done. They can accept a payment. They can approve it. And they can proceed. But this takes several steps. With Wallet Connect Pay, we actually have the ability to actually have a flow with the point of sale that actually scan a QR code, displays a single prompt in your wallet, and you just pay, and everything happens behind the scenes. And it's also a single integration that can be improved over time. So merchants don't have to be worried about, is this version 1, version 2, version 3?
