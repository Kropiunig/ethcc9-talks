# Regulation as Infrastructure: Building Trust & Scale in Digital Asset Ma
**Ophelia Snyder** (21shares) --- Monroe Stage

Okay. Well, hello, everyone. Sorry about that. So I'm here to talk to you guys about regulation. And to start, regulation is infrastructure. And I want to start by saying something that might even be a little uncomfortable in this room. I love regulation. I know, but stay with me. My name is Ophelia Snyder. I co-founded a company called 21Shares in 2018, built the business from zero to $12 billion in AUM, about $175 million, $200 million in ARR. 120-person team with offices and regulatory infrastructure in three countries and built to a significant exit last year. So how did we do that? We did that by building regulated products in regulated structures for regulated investors at a time when most of the industry thought that was boring and probably a terrible idea. And I'm here to tell you today, it was actually the only way to do it. And more importantly, I'm here to tell you that the thing that felt like a constraint back then is now the single biggest competitive advantage available to anyone building in this space. Regulation isn't a gate. It's not a ceiling. It's a base layer that everything else gets built on. And fundamentally, that's what this talk is actually about. So before we get into the argument, I want to acknowledge something directly. I'm guessing that a lot of people in this room are not new to this industry and likely built things during years where the word regulation was largely synonymous with enforcement actions, when the only communication you ever got from a regulator was a subpoena, a lawsuit, a letter if you were lucky telling you you were doing something wrong and that you absolutely needed to stop. And it was a time when the rules of the game, if you can even really call them rules, were build fast, get big, get acquired, or get distributed enough that nobody can touch you. And while obviously that's not a great way of doing things, I was in that environment. I completely get what it produced. What it produced is a very reasonable and rational reflex that regulation is inherently adversarial. It's there to protect incumbents. It's there to punish innovation. It's there to slow down anyone who is building things or moving fast enough to do anything that matters. That reflex wasn't wrong. It was accurate and an expression of extremely real conditions. But now it's more of a trap. And here's what's actually true about how technology, including financial technology, gets adopted at scale. Not just crypto, but everywhere. The internet did not eliminate banks. It became infrastructure inside of banks that enabled their core business. Mobile payments did not eliminate cards. It became a layer on top of cards that allowed them to actually operate more effectively. If you want to go broader, streaming did not eliminate television. It became a new distribution model for content. And digital assets and on-chain finance are not going to eliminate finance. They are the natural next layer and the next evolution of this industry. And when that happens, if you kind of look at the history of financial innovation, it really plays out the same way, which is that adoption actually follows regulatory clarity, not the reverse. And I was listening to some of the remarks earlier and they were kind of getting at that. You know, we've said the, you know, adoption is here, mass adoption is here, the institutions are here or they're coming for a long time. But ultimately, you really can't have that until you clear this hurdle, which is the need for regulatory clarity. Regulatory clarity isn't the gate at the front door. It isn't, you know, a gate you need to go through or something you just need to get over with. It's actually the concrete under the building. It is the base that absolutely everything in financial industry is actually in most industries is built on top of. And right now, for the first time in the history of digital assets, we're actually pouring that concrete. We're laying that foundation. And I think that's something that's quite undervalued. Now, let me give you a mental model I've been using for years that I think is genuinely helpful here. You can think of financial regulation as a layer cake. And I think once you kind of look at it this way, a lot of what we're seeing in the market starts to make, I think, more sense. The bottom layer, the base, is classification. Answering the really kind of somewhat semantic question of what is this thing? Who's responsible for it? Those are often pretty linked. I think in, you know, most of us have been part of these conversations, right? Is crypto a commodity? Is it a security? Is it a currency? Is it a payment instrument? And while yes, that may sound like a very dry semantic question, it's actually not at all. It's the basis of the entire game. You cannot regulate something without first defining it. Until you have a clear and maybe most importantly legally stable answer to that question, you can't build anything else on top. You can't write custodial rules for something that you can't define. You can't build a product structure on top of something that has no legal category. You can't pass an institutional investment committee, not one, not anywhere in the world on the basis of legal ambiguity. So next, there's a middle layer, which I like to think of as governance, which is once you know what the thing is and you know who's responsible for making the rules of the road, the next question is, okay, well, how does this thing actually operate? And what rules are we really going to put in place here? And that can be a pretty wide range of things. That is issuings and custody and distribution and trading and reporting and who can access and how they can access. But that's still only part of the story, right? And it's pretty linearly built on that categorization. So now you have what is it and what is the rules you're trying to actually make for it? The top layer is interoperability. And for me, it's actually, I think, the most important one. How does this new thing that we've now defined and built rules for interact with every other piece of legislation that's ever been written? So a way to think about that is, how does it connect to everything else? How does a tokenized bond interact with a untokenized bond? Does it change the rules for bonds? Are they both the same thing? Is it now just bonds all have one set of rules? How does a stable coin sit inside of existing bank regulation? How does on-chain settlement work with existing settlement infrastructure and, you know, regulatory requirements around transfer agency and record keeping? That's where regulation goes from being kind of a standalone thing to becoming part of a cohesive framework. Now, the problem with crypto is that for most of its life, it actually never even had a base layer. You couldn't define it. You couldn't legally categorize it in any meaningful way. Every regulator had a different answer. The SEC said it was a security. The CFTC said a commodity. Treasury said currency, kind of sometimes, but not always. And no one agreed. And because no one agreed, and this is not just a U.S. story, no one agreed anywhere. Institutions couldn't act, not because they were afraid, and I think that can often be the narrative, is they were afraid to kind of step into this space, but because actually they had no legal ground to stand on. And that's really important for these larger institutions. Now, there's been a lot of talk in the last couple of years, and quite frankly, especially in the last couple of months, around stable coins. And I want to make a point around sort of the stable coin ecosystem specifically. Rules for stable coins aren't actually about stable coins for the most part. When you define what a tokenized dollar is or a tokenized Swiss franc or euro, you are actually laying the groundwork and the legal language that will eventually govern everything else. Who can issue it? How it's backed? What recourse investors actually have against the people who are issuing and manufacturing these products? And that is going to impact how tokenized stocks work, how on-chain bonds work, how commodity trading is going to work, how we can bring real world assets on chain, and ultimately how we can develop new on-chain financial products. And it's a wonderful example of how every piece of base layer clarity that you develop sets the precedent for everything that comes after it. The base layer is what everything else depends on. Get it wrong and the whole cake collapses. So, which brings me to what's actually happening right now. And I want to spend some real time talking about this, not because people haven't heard about these things, but because I think it's being severely and significantly underestimated, even by people who are following this space closely. For the first time in the history of this industry, we have a serious coordinated regulatory framework that's built across multiple major jurisdictions simultaneously. Not enforcement, not hostile ambiguity, not regulatory guidance that contradicts itself from one agency to the next or from one country to the next. Actual usable frameworks. We can talk about the Genius Act first. This is the first serious federal U.S. attempt to define what a stable coin is. Reserve requirements, licensing pathways, oversight structures, basic stuff. Again, matters not because of stable coins. It's helping to establish that base layer language that will govern everything tokenized that comes after it. And maybe most kind of interestingly, it's one of the first examples we've seen in the U.S. of a piece of legislation that's being developed with genuine industry input. And that change of posture is quite significant as well. So it shows that the era of regulation handed down after the fact is over, at least for now. Then you have clarity, which goes after the hardest question directly. What's the line between a commodity and a security in digital assets? That question has essentially paralyzed major institutions that are interested in being in this space for a decade.
