So this talk is about what we've learned in discussions with institutions about privacy. So if you're in this room, you probably are already sold on privacy, but just to level set, blockchain is the only financial system where everything is completely visible. Every transaction is visible to everyone. So even if your bank account is private and your paycheck is private, once you move on-chain, everything is public. For individuals, this means that anyone with a block explorer can effectively reconstruct your entire financial history, which is not great. But for institutions, it's even worse. It means that volumes, relationships, payroll, trading strategies, everything is completely visible to the world and worse to your competitors. So when we talk to financial institutions, they make it very clear that not having a way to manage visibility is a non-starter for any meaningful engagement with blockchain. So you should say this is easy. We've been working on privacy and blockchain for the past 10 years. We have, you know, TornadoCache, ZCash, and so on and so forth. But not exactly. So, you know, we've been working on privacy and crypto in a very kind of like ideological, cypherpunk way in which we're promoting complete financial freedom, which is awesome. But for this next wave of adoption, we really need institutions. So we need them to feel comfortable bringing their assets on-chain. We want them to make payroll. We want them to accept merchant payments. We want them to engage with blockchain in a meaningful way. So for these institutions, privacy means something a bit different. It means managing visibility in a way that gives them control, allows them to stay both competitive and compliant. And so this mistake that we've made is really building privacy that is absolute. But what institutions are telling us is that privacy is, in fact, a spectrum. Privacy is not binary. It's a spectrum with an enormous number of permutations. Jurisdiction varies. So a regulated asset that is issued in the U.S. adheres to a very different regulatory regime than one in an equivalent one in Germany. We have variants by use cases. So the type of privacy you need for payments is different from remittances, lending, or trading. And we also have a lot of variants in technology. So, you know, we talk a lot about zero-knowledge cryptography here, but ZK, FHE, MPCTs, all of them bring different types of performance to the table, different types of privacy guarantees, and what they can actually hide. So the real problem is that we've been treating privacy as basically like a single feature, but it's actually this multidimensional design space. And so not recognizing this privacy spectrum has led to what I like to call privacy dead ends. And so, you know, when we started looking at privacy for Stellar a couple of years ago, we realized that most privacy solutions out there today fall into one of two failure modes or dead ends. One of them is the all or nothing failure mode. And so these are privacy solutions that have basically two modes, either full transparency or full privacy, nothing in between. So this can take the shape of a privacy, of a pool, of a specific pool. This can take the shape of an L2 or an app chain. But regardless, the premise is all the same, all or nothing. This is not good enough for institutions. They need more room and nuance for regulated financial institutions. The other failure mode is the permission chain, which is a bit of a sensitive topic here. I've been in crypto for almost nine years, and I think that permission chains are something that every couple of years, you know, like new ones emerge, they die off, and then suddenly they reemerge. And I think that one disturbing thing we've seen recently is there are more permission chains emerging. And, you know, this time around, it's on the premise of privacy. Now, yes, they're private. They're private because it's a database on your computer, right? And so, you know, what we're seeing is that institutions are looking for open participation network. They see the value of interoperability. They see the value of composability, verifiability, and so permission chains or centralized databases are a dead end. Okay, so how do you avoid these type of privacy dead ends? We think the answer is simple by having a clear architectural separation in the privacy stack. So transparency should come first. The base layer should stay open and auditable. This might come as a surprise, given that this is a talk about privacy, but transparency is a core value proposition of blockchain for institutions. The fact that an executive at an asset management company can go into a block explorer and see the circulating supply of an asset and see the high level distribution, that is not a bug, that is a feature. They love it. We want to make sure that we provide this value moving forward. And so we suggest on top of that, layering in building blocks for privacy. So the base layer should provide the primitives that enable privacy without enshrining a specific privacy protocol. We've seen L1s try to enshrine privacy protocols. It doesn't end well. Sometimes it's buggy. Sometimes it's just obsolete by the time it comes out. And so what we did at Stellar is take a building blocks approach. We actually launched the X-Ray protocol upgrade a couple months ago, which introduced a very rich set of zero knowledge cryptography primitives so that you can come and build whatever privacy protocol you want on top. And that's the final layer, privacy protocols on top. Different protocols for different privacy guarantees, different administrative controls, and different underlying tech. And most importantly, there will be many privacy protocols, and that's okay. So we keep talking about these different privacy protocols for different use cases. Let's give some examples. And so I want to focus on private payments because there's a lot of interesting stuff happening in DeFi in general, private trading, et cetera, but these are still a ways out, and payments are by far the most important, most immediate use case. And in private payments, we're seeing two major families of privacy happening these days. One of them is confidential tokens. So confidential tokens hide the what. They hide the payment amounts and they hide the balances. But the sender and receivers are still visible. So this is great for when counterparty relationships are known. For example, in a payroll situation, everyone knows that I'm employed by SDF, so that's not a problem to disclose. But with that said, the amounts and balances are. So there are a bunch of different implementations of this. We at Stellar are part of the Confidential Token Association with others like OpenZeppelin and Zama. And we're in the process of building an implementation specifically for that. And the nice thing about confidential tokens is that even though they deliver a fairly narrow sense of privacy, it's still something that is usable, has value, and more importantly, it's scalable, and the compliance story is fairly easy because the counterparties are known. The second family we're seeing, the second family of private payments are privacy pools. So these are privacy, private protocols that hide both the what and the who. So funds are mixed together so that senders and receivers are not shown. They're hidden. And this provides anonymity on top of confidentiality. So some notable examples of that are Oxbow in the Ethereum ecosystem that have been doing a really great job. We've also seen Nethermind launch Stellar private payments on Stellar, which is kind of like a building block to build privacy pools on Stellar. And privacy pools have a much stronger


privacy guarantee, but they inherently mix funds. So that's dangerous because it means that legitimate funds can be commingled with illicit funds. That's a problem, but there are ways to tackle this that I will cover. So this demonstrates how we have different privacy guarantees. Again, privacy is a spectrum. And so the question is, how do we build administrative tools that allow us to control to enable the compliance with these protocols and others? So at Stellar, we've been working with various builders in the space to define a compliance menu of opt-in administrative control. Now, the asterisk in this in compliance is deliberate. I am not a lawyer. And also the space of privacy and blockchains is so new that we don't really know what compliance looks like. So these are the administrative controls we're starting to see as requirements. This is not a comprehensive list. And the idea is to have these as configurable and opt-in as needed. So the first thing we're seeing, the first control, is selective disclosure, usually using view keys. So the user chooses to reveal specific transaction details to specific parties. This allows the user to show a specific source of funds on demand. So for example, if I'm depositing into Coinbase, Coinbase wants to know where these funds are coming from. I can show them a cryptographically secure source of funds. It's a very powerful primitive. It's existed for a while, but unfortunately, we haven't really seen great protocols built on top of it. But I believe we're going to see more of that coming soon. The next administrative control in this compliance menu is non-selective disclosure. So in this scheme, a third-party auditor is able to hold a key that view all transactions within a scope. So that means that an issuer or a pool operator has these keys, and if law enforcement, for example, needs to trace a specific transaction or a specific user, they basically have like a door to knock on, so they know who to look for. The next administrative control is association sets. So this was pioneered by the privacy pools paper a few years ago by Vitalik, Amin, and others, utilized by OXBO. And the idea is that this addresses the need to basically have some sort of filter on who can participate in a pool. So that even though a pool is commingling funds, we know that everyone in this pool have went through some minimal set of compliance checks. Then the question is, what happens if someone passes the compliance check, but then later was proven to be problematic, or the funds had like some problematic source? And so for that, we're seeing various solutions that look like forced transparent withdrawals. So the idea is, basically, if you're removed from the association set, you're forced to withdraw, but not in a private way. So your privacy guarantees are not maintained, but you're still in control of your funds. OXBO calls this rage quit, but we're seeing a lot of these types of use cases coming up. And the last one is clawback. So yes, clawbacks are a bit triggering for us in crypto, but you know, tokenization is at full speed. Regulated asset issuers require clawback. And if we want them to participate in a meaningful way, then yes, we need to have clawback capabilities. Again, this is a privacy compliance menu. You don't have to choose all of them.
