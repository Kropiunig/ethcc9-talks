# From 30M to 300M: The Engineering Behind Ethereum’s Gas Li
**Toni Wahrstaetter** (Ethereum Foundation) --- Redford Stage

Hello everyone, my name is Tony. I'm with the Ethereum Foundation, happy to be here. And today's topic will basically be scaling. So before we get into the topic, I want to quickly tell you some history, how we got where we are today, and also in the end of the talk, looking a bit into the future. So getting into it, how we got where we are today. This is basically the daily gas use summed up per day. And you can see that Ethereum has been scaling quite a lot since the beginning, especially the most important and most significant event that you see on this chart is the London hard fork where we shipped 1559. And since then, since the London hard fork, we had the merge that very stabilized the daily gas usage. But since then, not much happened basically. And the London hard fork allowed us to go from 50 million gas limit to 30 million, so basically already doubling the gas limit. But then for three years, we weren't really scaling. And because of that, the community developed this appetite for scaling, and more and more people were pushing for increasing the gas limit, and nothing happened for three years. And then at the end of this chart, at the very right-hand side, we entered 2024. And this was when the people basically reached out and wanted to increase the gas limit. So this is from an Ethereum Foundation Reddit AMA where some person asked Vitalik, like, hey, can we increase the gas limit? And Vitalik very reasonably said, yeah, it should be possible. A moderate gas limit increase is possible, reasoning about Moore's law. And this is basically how everything started. Other people, like Dankrad, for example, who was also very much pushing for a higher gas limit, they were still calling for patience because at the same time, Ethereum was just about to ship the Dankoon hard fork that introduced blobs to the network. And as you might know, blobs already added a lot of data. So people need to download and propagate all the data. And this meant if we increased the gas limit at the same time, we would also increase the network packages by quite a lot. So we were still very hesitant about increasing the gas limit at that time, but over time, more and more people pushed for it. The Sepolia incident was one of those events that also slowed things down a bit because we stumbled across a bug that was caused by a big block. So you could submit a block that was going over the 10 megabytes. And what happened on Sepolia was Geth basically forking off. And this was a very serious bug because it could have happened on mainnet and it would have been very difficult to recover from such a bug. And fortunately, it happened only on Sepolia. But it just showed us, like, just increasing the gas limit naively by just pumping some numbers is not the way to go. Then continuing, we entered the Pump the Gas era. And many of you might remember this era. Here we see Paolo and Martin Koppelmann from Gnosis, like, pushing for higher gas limit. Mariano.eth launched PumpTheGas.org. And this basically kind of created this great momentum for the community to eventually push for higher gas limits. And the community wanted to go to 60 million, at the minimum 45 million. And there were other people that had, like, a different opinion, you know? Just calling out this guy here who wanted to even lower the gas limit because, of course, we have rollups now, right? So let's just solve the rollup fragmentation. We can even lower the gas limit. And to be clear, you know, this is like one and a half years ago. And I was not alone. Many people reached out and were like, yeah, actually, that's the way to go. Let's make sure we have small blocks on Ethereum. And instead, we increase the blob count. But yeah, I was convinced that this is not only a controversial take, but maybe a bad take. Then we got more and more people, Dankrad, FIDA, Sazle. Many people were pushing hard for increasing the gas limit. But then reality hit us hard. Because what we found is if you push the gas limit beyond 39 million, we would basically break the network again. Some clients were not yet ready for gas limit increases. And what could happen is we pumped the gas limit and some nodes would just fork off. So this was very bad. We were basically confronted with reality once again and realized, okay, we actually need to focus more on that topic. Laying the path for further gas limit increases. So what happened here, we suggested the 36 million gas limit in this blog post and wanted to wait until EIP 7623 is shipped. So EIP 7623 increased the call data cost, which directly led to smaller worst-case blocks. And this is quite nice because it gave us some room to then increase the gas limit. Justin was basically the first to then increase the gas limit to 36 million on his validator. And many people followed. And in the following weeks, we eventually got to the 36 million, which was already a very small step, but still quite a success story. And since then, we got way more professional when dealing with the gas limit. We had dashboards like this one. We launched gas limit tests on testnets first, first on local testnets, then on the public testnets. And in the end, there were much more people just looking into it. For example, here you can see a blog post from the ETHPandaOps team, basically our DevOps team. And they were focusing a lot on what do we need to do in order to make sure that we can further increase the gas limit from 36 million to 60 million. And in the end, we even had an EIP that set the default gas limit to 60 million. So this is very powerful because as soon as you update your node, you don't run any custom flags that set the gas limit. You will just go with the 60 million. So this eventually got us to 60 million where we are today. And this is a chart from ETHIscan where you can see the stepwise increase that happened over the recent year, basically going from 36 million to 45 million and then to 60 million. And what all we did so far was basically squeezing out everything that we can from clients. So there was no major change that allowed us to increase the gas limit. It was more like CoreDevs just grinding hard, trying to squeeze out every little bit of efficiency from the clients without actually doing protocol changes that would support further gas limit increases. And then at some point, we were just asking ourselves, how do we continue from there on? Because clients are tired optimizing their clients. And at some point, we need to do something to actually scale the chain. So there were a bunch of options, right? Other chains were looking into parallelization. Terrence was just talking about EPBS, which is basically using the slack time in the slot for something more useful. What about ZKVMs, right? Everyone has heard about ZKVMs. Are they already around the corner? Can we use them? And the same applies to network optimizations. Like, can we, without changing the core protocol, can we just make things on the networking side more efficient to scale the gas limit? And one thing was very clear from the beginning. We will not just increase the node requirements because this will be the shortcut here. A very simple shortcut, but at the same time, it could mean we sacrifice some of the core properties of Ethereum. So just increasing the node requirements was always, like, not a very viable option. Even though it would be reasonable in our time, might be doable, but we were not ready for that yet. So this confronted us with this situation, like, what do we do? Like, do we go all in the crops, basically censorship resistance, open source, privacy, security, all the values Ethereum stands for, or do we want to focus on scaling? And the community was very divided. But fortunately, there was this very nice trick that we haven't really thought about it. And it's basically this. You can use your thumb, your pinky finger, and then you get both of it. You can get crops and you can get scaling. And this is where we, in the end, ended up with doing both. So what happened was the core devs, the community, they really mobilized behind scaling the L1. So scaling the L1 was suddenly this big theme, and everyone was on board. Also the Ethereum Foundation had, with Tomasz, laying out those three buckets, scale L1, scale L2, improve UX. So there was a lot of momentum at that time to just increase the gas limit. And in the end, this is where we are basically today. We decided to ship two EIPs for Clemsterdam. So those are the two headliner EIPs. We just heard about EPBS, EIP 7732. And the other one is EIP 7928 block level access list. And in my talk, I want to mainly focus on block level access list. So let's dive deeper into 7928, block-level access list, which will be the EIP shipped in Amsterdam for parallelization. There are four problems we have today with parallelizing transactions. First of all, there are unknown dependencies. Transactions conflict with each other. They touch the same state and so on. Then dynamic access patterns. Transactions could branch into random directions based on the runtime, right? So when your transaction lands at the top of the block, it could do something completely different than if you would place it in the middle of the block or at the end of the block. And this makes optimistically parallelizing transactions very challenging. And finally, there's EIP2930 that some of you might remember, like the transaction access lists, but unfortunately, it's optional and it's not really accurate because you as a user, you can't really know what your transaction is going to do because as said, it might depend on the runtime. So to solve those four problems, we came up with EIP7928 and the TLDR was basically, let's introduce parallelism to Ethereum. So looking at how it works. So this is how block validation looks like today. You download the block, you propagate it, and then you usually do some IO. You go to disk, you load state, you execute, you go to disk again, and so on. And at the end of the block, you calculate the state root because you need the state root in your header. And this is basically what we can't parallelize as of today because we don't know what is going to happen. With block-level access list, we suddenly can. So basically, you download the block, and then IO, execution, post-state root calculation can all be done in parallel. And this allows us to scale the gas limit in the end quite massively. Looking at how transactions are executed here, exactly what I mentioned earlier. You go to disk, you execute, you go to disk, and so on. And with block-level access lists, we get to a picture like the following, where you have one thread that fetches everything you need in that block from the disk, and the rest of the threads are basically busy already executing. And it doesn't matter anymore if transactions have conflicts with each other because the block-level access list allows you to resolve those conflicts and get you to perfect parallelization. So in the worst case, you can always parallelize transactions, no matter what. And this is what the block access list contains. It's two things, state locations, which means, what do I need from state for executing that block? And the second thing, which is even more important, is the status, or to be more granular, a post-transaction status. So you get exactly the values that you would find on state after executing a certain transaction. And because the values after one transaction are the value before another transaction, you can use that as basically almost like if you had the state for each transaction available. And this allows you, in the end, to execute transactions in any arbitrary order. So the block-level access list looks like the following. I won't go too deep into this, but basically it's a very simple structure where you have accounts. Each account is mapped to changes, like the slot changes, storage keys, the balance changes, nonce changes, and code changes. That makes up all the state. And then coming from the slot changes, you can see that a slot change consists again of a storage key. So you have account, then storage keys, and then the exact mapping to which transaction changes the state and what is the value after that transaction. So the block-level access list is essentially also a status for the block. And the benefits we get are those four. We get execution parallelism, we get batch prefetch, we get parallel state root computation, and one that I haven't mentioned so far that I like a lot because I think it's a super powerful use case is faster snap sync. So you can basically replace the get trie node method in the healing phase for snap sync with block-level access lists. And then you would do, for those of you that are familiar with snap sync, you would do the skeleton sync, you would download the block bodies, and usually you would start the healing phase in that time. But here you would still do healing, but instead of trie node fetching, you would apply block-level access lists. And this is a major simplification for the snap sync process.
