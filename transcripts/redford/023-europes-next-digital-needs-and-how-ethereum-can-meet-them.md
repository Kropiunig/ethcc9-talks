# Europe’s Next Digital Needs and How Ethereum Can Meet Them
**Kelly Roegies** (European Aluminium) --- Redford Stage

Hi, good afternoon, everyone. My name is Kelly, and I'm sure you checked the program and you saw European Aluminium as my organization, and you thought, what the hell? Well, so before I start, let me give you a quick intro. I've been working in Brussels on EU policy for the past nine years, translating what EU policy means in practice for the aluminium companies in Europe that need to implement it. I'm also a fellow at the European Decentralization Institute, which is a fairly new think tank that looks into how decentralization can contribute to EU policy goals, such as digital sovereignty. And what I've realized over the past nine years is this, and this is kind of a hot take, right, that, yes, we talk a lot about EU regulation, how it stifles innovation. The EU tends to overregulate, and that in many cases is true. But if you look at EU legislation closely, it can, in some cases, also be a product roadmap for what still needs to be built. So in Web3, we ask a lot of times, how can we get institutions and enterprises to adopt Ethereum, to adopt permissionless blockchain? So I will argue that because of legislation, this question, in part, is starting to answer itself because right now the EU is forcing into existence through legislation with legal obligations and deadlines attached entire new digital infrastructure. And today I will focus on three digital legislations across digital identity, supply chains, and AI. And I will tell you the gaps they create, who needs them filled, and why the requirements these legislations create point to permissionless blockchain and Ethereum in particular. So I'm not a technical person, right, so I'll leave the technical solutions to the devs in this audience. But I would say let's start with identity. So under eIDAS 2.0, the EU is building a public identity layer. By the end of 2026, all 27 EU member states will be required to roll out a digital identity wallet to its roughly 450 million citizens. By the end of the year, public services are required to accept those wallets. And by the end of 2027, banks, telcos, and other private services with strong authentication are also required to accept the digital identity wallet. And in this wallet are things like your university diploma, your driving license, your national ID, all issued as verifiable credentials. Now, the EU legislation requires all parties, so wallets, providers, credential issuers, and verifiers, to ensure that there is no tracking possible of how and when users authenticate. But the problem is that no one has quite figured out how to enforce this technically. So the regulation, the EU credential formats that they've chosen, let you select only part of your data, which is of course good, right? This is selective disclosure, it's very important for privacy. But the problem is it doesn't stop a determined actor from piecing everything together across different services. So unlinkability is not guaranteed, and that is a huge privacy concern, because you can have someone, a malicious actor, conspiring with services and kind of create a whole profile on you, and then it becomes, instead of a digital identity wallet that is useful for citizens, a mass surveillance tool. So this is incredibly important pitfall to avoid. So the EU has also recognized that unlinkability, full unlinkability across all platforms, requires cryptographic tools that aren't yet widely supported on the issuer side. So these wallets are going live very soon. Platforms will need to accept them, but they'll have a no-tracking obligation they can't yet fully comply to because the tools that exist there right now, they don't do that yet. So the wallets are going live with a compliance hole, which is a little bit ridiculous, right? So what is missing is the layer between the platform and the wallet. In the best case, the user presents a wallet to a service platform, and the platform, the wallet generates only a proof that proves the claim, such as, is this person 18? Yes or no, without sharing the personal identity and data underneath that claim, right? So this will prevent linkability across services. The platform only receives a check that this proof happens, and no personal identifiable information is stored by the platform. And this is very important. And of course, in Ethereum's ecosystem, we have ZK tooling. It's tested and used in Ethereum more than anywhere else. And you have already primitives such as Polygon ID, Self Protocol. I know the EF PSC team is also looking at ZK SDKs, also including for the digital identity wallets. But right now, there is not yet any SDK that is compliant with this eIDAS regulation. Even though across the world, other nations are working on it, I'm sure you've all heard last year about the example of Bhutan, right? That could be a great model for Europe, but we're not quite there yet. And I think also what is important to note that the EU, I mean, lost legislation, and there's different pieces here and there that are kind of interlinked with the identity issues. For example, under the EU's Digital Services Act, very large platforms such as TikTok are under immense pressure to ensure that minors cannot access their platforms. The European Commission wants them to put effective age assurance measures in place, and they're backing privacy-preserving approaches, of course, very strongly. So that could also be a use case for a ZK wallet-based flow. Now, my favorite topic, digital product passports. So I'm representing the manufacturing industry in my full-time role, right? So I'm closely involved with this. So basically, under the Ecodesign for Sustainable Products Regulation, as of February 2027, every electric vehicle battery placed on the EU market will need a digital product passport, which is essentially a structured record of specific claims, depending on which sector, which claims. So for batteries, it will be things like recycled content data, the origin of the raw materials, emissions associated to the production of those, and so on. And the intention under this regulation is that eventually nearly every product placed on the EU market will need to have a digital passport. So like a physical identifier attached to the product, like a QR code, then you can scan it and you have the whole digital record of a product from across the entire lifecycle there. So the next wave will be after batteries, textiles, apparel, furniture, tires, toys, metals, including aluminium and steel, electronics, and so on. So you can imagine just how many companies will need to comply with this because also EU importers will need to eventually have EU exporters. No, sorry. Those exporting to the EU will eventually need a digital product passport. Now, the issue with these product passports is not the logging of the claims, right? Because this can be very easily done with a centralized enterprise solution. But the problem is the typical one that blockchain can solve. It's the coordination problem between actors that do not trust each other. Because as you can see here on screen, one product can involve many actors, dozens of actors from across the globe. For a battery, for example, you can have several miners for each of the raw materials, processors, cell makers, and so on, all the way to the recycler. And each of these actors need to add claims to the same record. And the problem is, of course, these actors don't always trust each other because they can also be competitors in some markets. So I think what needs to be built...


Instead, first of all, a ZK compliance claim API. So where a supply chain actor can submit a claim, gets a receipt, and that can be anchored as a cryptographic commitment on Ethereum with a timestamp and a hash, so that someone who wants to check the claim, be it a regulator, be it an end-use consumer, can check the claim without having to go back to the original verifier, and of course that claim cannot be altered. Now, in supply chain traceability, there's also certain data that is commercially too sensitive to share with your competitors, or you don't even want to share with end-use customers, but you might need to share it with regulators. Think about, for example, carbon calculations, carbon emission calculations, and so on. So again, here, you could think about having ZK proofs for those commercially sensitive issues. And the second product, if you will, is a registry of supply chain actors. The EU will build a central registry, but I think a decentralized registry that makes a lot of sense here, where you have verifiable identity across the value chain, and also that this identity can be portable, because it could very well be that you will need to comply also with other regulations, like the Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism, in which you need to prove the carbon emissions of your product if you import it into the EU. And this does not exist yet. And of course, we have already Ethereum's attestation service, which could be very well suited to this product. And I think what is great is that there's already a lot of procurement happening across all those industries that are in scope in wave one, two, and three. I know the aluminium industry is looking into it, and there's, of course, permissioned blockchains being built by some big industrial companies, but also many companies are interested in permissionless alternatives, and there's already people building on Ethereum. There's a company, for example, called Circularize that is in metals and mining that is making digital identity, no, sorry, digital product passport solutions with ZK built on Ethereum. And I think this is a huge market and it's one of the, I think, applications of blockchain that's not often mentioned, but that could be huge. And then finally, the EU AI Act, which is already in force. And as of August, 2026, everyone that is categorized as high-risk AI must log everything over the entire system's lifetime. That makes it easy to reconstruct what the system did, when, and based on what inputs. And as you can see on screen, a lot of systems fall into high-risk categories, credit scoring, CV screening, and so on. It's a very broad category. And there is no specification in the legislation for how to best track the system's lifetime, but temper-evident logs are the best legally defensive way to do that. So imagine what this means in practice. You have, for example, an HR tech company and a decision, like a candidate contests a decision, right, six months later. And then the company needs to prove, you know, what inputs it processed, which model version it used, what decision came out of that, and importantly, that the record, the log, hasn't been touched since. And of course, normal enterprise logging does not give you this because anyone that runs the infra can adjust the record. So that's why I think a decentralized solution makes the most sense here. And what is also important to note is that many of these decisions made by these high-risk AI systems will constitute a highly sensitive personal data, such as a hiring decision, but also a loan outcome. And under the EU's GDPR Act, everyone has the right to ask their personal data to be erased under Article 17. And this is, of course, in direct tension with the temper-evident logs that should not be editable under the EU AI Act. So I think the most elegant solution to solve that is to anchor only a cryptographic fingerprint of anonymized log data on-chain and never reveal the personal data itself. This will check both boxes, right, both regulations, and the right to erase your will be satisfied. So two products that can be built here, a compliance anchoring service, which connects to existing enterprise logging structure because no one wants to rebuild the stack from scratch, and anchors verifiable fingerprints to a shared neutral layer, such as Ethereum, and could also generate audit reports that regulators can verify. Again, you can look at EAS here. And second, a model version registry, which can prove which AI model made which decision and that no single party can alter. And this can be done on ESA with L2, like Base, which makes it also very cost efficient. And I can imagine that such a model version registry will be very useful beyond AI compliance, AI Act compliance. Think about an AI agent accountability registry or training data provenance. And before I close, I wanted to give a few words on how to make institutional and enterprise buyers say yes, because I think an issue a lot of technically strong projects have is that they lead with the technical features when they first talk to procurement teams. And I think you have to always be mindful to use their vocabulary, not yours. And if you're talking to compliance teams about compliance solutions, talk in the regulations language, right? So instead of immediately saying, well, we use zero-knowledge proofs to preserve privacy, the compliance teams are not technical people. They will have no idea what it means. Say something like, our solution satisfies the unlinkability requirements under eIDAS. And always describe first the why, not the how, right? So lead with the problem and show how your technology can solve the compliance issues they already have, and then the technical discussion will follow when they bring their tech people on board. But for example, for a digital product passports, the first person you might speak to to present your solution could be the head of sustainability and they have no interest in really knowing the technical details. So tell them the why, not the how first. And don't be a decentralization maxi, right? Let's leave that for our internal discussions, but show them rather how centralized solutions that they might have currently in place or that they are considering, how they will not work. So show them a compliance gap, like for example, there's no full unlinkability possible with a centralized solution and so on. So kind of make the case for decentralization without mentioning even the word decentralization, right? Because that can scare them off a little bit. They don't need to believe in decentralization. They don't need to believe in Ethereum. They're looking for a solution to their compliance issue and something they can defend internally to their boss. So be mindful of that. And I think when you do that, and I think what we have seen over the past 20 minutes is that this is a little bit different from any other products building this space in that no one needs convincing they have a problem, right? There is already a problem created by legislation that they have to solve with deadlines attached and with heavy fines up to 7% of their global turnover if they don't comply. So that is kind of an easy way in. And the common requirements across all these three legislations that I've just mentioned is that they all require shared infrastructure in which multiple actors will use, but no one should really be in charge of. So this is not an ideological preference, right? It's more of a procurement constraint. And of course they need something that is publicly verifiable, highly secure, difficult to tamper with. And this is exactly the kind of properties that Ethereum can provide. It is publicly verifiable, extremely hard to tamper with. No single vendor or institution is running it. It's securing billions in global value and it's maintained by a global ecosystem and has no downtime since running. So I think the opportunity here in closing.


The thing is to recognize where regulation is creating the requirements that Ethereum can provide solutions to, and then you will see, I think, that often Ethereum is already structurally aligned with where the EU is going. Thank you.
