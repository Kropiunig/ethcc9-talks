# Designing Coherence: Aligning Ethereum Stakeholders Through Ph
**Dan Ligocky** (Duct Tape / ETHPrague) --- Redford Stage

Okay, okay, okay. Hi everyone. Hey. My name is Dan and I'm going to talk about how physical environments influence the way we connect and why this is especially important within Ethereum. To give you a quick intro, before Ethereum, I spent 15 years designing these temporary environments, different ones, from underground parties to large-scale events, film, music. I did a lot of experiential stuff and conferences. But then, a friend of mine and my co-founder brought me to a rave. And this was in Lisbon, 2021, and I was amazed from what I've seen. I saw smart, intelligent, open-minded people at a professionally run conference during the daytime. And then I stumbled upon the same group in the evening. And what I discovered was that these two worlds can collide. And I think this is something special we have within the ecosystem. And the energy felt different. It was the same people discussing the same things, but there it clicked. So I decided to build these environments intentionally, and I spent five years designing Ethereum-focused events. We established ETH Prague conference and Ethereum Zurich. And a part of that, we've been working for teams, protocols, and foundations around the globe, setting up over two dozens of events at different countries. But what is not important is the scale. It's how the rooms work. And we have also something special within Ethereum, and that's we coexist within parallel worlds. It's different people with different incentives who come from different backgrounds. And all these people come to coordinate and build trust among each other. So as opposed to centralized organization when decisions are assigned and there's like hierarchy, within Ethereum, decisions have to be discovered and alignment must emerge. So the problem with Ethereum is not the technology, but the coordination. And we all know this from our daily lives. We use all these tools that enable us to connect, but we end up overloaded with information and what we see at the best is digital interpretation of people with who we talk to. If it comes to written communication, then these days we cannot even be sure that it was actually written by the person who's sending us. So there's high information and low alignment. The rooms actually compress coordination and trust. And this is especially important for what we do. Because if you shape the environment in a correct way, then you achieve behavior that leads to a faster coordination and it accelerates the whole ecosystem. So what works? It's mostly the shared experiences because these have capacity to bind people together. And the more unorthodox and unexpected experiences we create, the better bonds among the people. Say a couple of years ago, we went to a team building to catacombs in Paris. And this was especially difficult for those of us who didn't like the confined spaces, but we spent eight hours there. And I'm absolutely sure that no one from the group will ever forget each other. At DevConnect in Buenos Aires, we opened up the week with a tango performance, which created context. And for the locals, it's brought in pride of the heritage they have. And for everyone else, it was context of the space because like we travel a lot to different countries and the context is all we have. Otherwise, all the experience would blur into each other. We also do at ETH Prague creative workshops to form the teams ahead of the hackathons, some urban walks, sound baths, but you can do whatever you want. And the intensity of the experience is what shapes and creates the bonds between people. Then it's also the contrast. And at ETH Prague, you can go to dinner at a palace one day, but the other day you end up with a beer can being the cleanest thing in your head because you are at the rave. And this especially work because we come from different environments and switching between context does not just make the events memorable, but it also makes the participants feel at home at some point. And when it comes to the venues, and this is especially important, what we tend to do at Ductape is we tend to pick landmarks instead of convention spaces because we want to create something unexpected. Say in Buenos Aires, we did this event at Planetario Galileo Galilei, and we used the dome for artistic projection of the team's vision. And this created a platform for the ideas to really land. In Zurich, it took us three years to get access to the main building in the historic city center, the building where Albert Einstein went to teach. And this completely changed the way how people show up and the quality of the talks and everything. Next month, we are staging ETH Prague at this UNESCO-grade art nouveau building in the heart of the city where I'm from, Prague. And what we're gonna do, we're gonna place the hackathon inside the main concert hall, and we're gonna let people to stay overnight, something which has never happened in the 100 plus years history of the space. Also, it's very important how we pick the people to attend because you don't curate guest lists, you create the outcomes. And a couple of examples what we achieved once we staged this like weird high contrast event within different environments. And we invited a journalist from CNBC. The next thing we know, we are live in the CNBC network and we have a feature article on the main page. Also, yes, last year, we invited a regular EU parliament member to ETH Prague and the co-author of MICA. And yesterday at this very event, it was unveiled that the president founded European Ethereum Institute to promote the technology as an infrastructure for the EU. So in a down market situation like this, I get the reasoning like why to cut down on events. They are expensive, hard to measure. Sometimes they don't scale. But it's important to understand that a part of the technology, Ethereum is human. And when blockchains coordinate machines, it's like the actually environments that coordinate humans. Thank you. Enjoy the lunch.
