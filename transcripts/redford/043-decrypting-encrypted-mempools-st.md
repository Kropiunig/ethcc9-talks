# Decrypting Encrypted Mempools: St
**Jonathan Passerat-Palmbach** (Imperial College London / Flashbots) --- Redford Stage

Hello everyone, and thank you for interrupting your lunch to come and talk to me. So today we're talking about encrypted mempools, and you might have seen that there's been a proposal for the latest headliners to push for encrypted mempools. It was interesting, I think, to look into what was in these proposals, what had been done before in the academic literature as well, and see what was the role of MEV in all of these concepts. So, as you probably know, in Ethereum today and most blockchains, we have this notion of MEV. And MEV is often pictured as like profit extraction, but it's a bit more complex than that. It's really about handling incentives. And in permissionless networks, we have to handle incentives in one way or another. The way we depict that at Flashbots usually is that you have a choice between three options as to how your MEV is being handled. You can make the choice or not make a choice, I think. If you don't make a choice, usually what happens is that MEV translates into spam in your networks. Or it can translate into latency races, which leads to centralization. So, in general, the option we favor is to run auctions. You might have heard the term of like order flow auctions that need to happen in order to control the MEV in a better way. And if we try to project that into what exists in the different forms of mempool and transaction ingress that we have today, in a way, the trilemma depicts this like, if you want fast and decentralized, usually that's what the public mempool gives you. If you are more interested in having private transactions, but still fast inclusion, that's the role of centralized builders usually. And in a way, the role that encrypted mempools hope to fill is to steer this trilemma towards more of the private but decentralized. And we'll see if we can do a bit better than that. So, as I said, the current workaround is usually to use private transaction lanes where instead of going through the public mempool, you send directly to a block builder, for example. And in that case, of course, you reduce the option for front-running, but you do add some trust in a specific builder, which, you know, can have some impacts in the way you are making your choices. You might not be happy, for example, with the geolocation constraints of a builder. Some builders have to sanction because of the region from which they operate. Interestingly, we see a different form of these transaction lanes now in what we call BuilderNet, which tries to, in a way, give you the best of both worlds with private, fast, but also decentralized because it's run by different operators. The magic here is, like, instead of trusting a given builder, you put your trust in T, so in the hardware supply chain in a way. And turns out that not everyone is happy with these guarantees, so emerged another option. What can you do? Well, potentially, you can encrypt everything, right? That's the fundamental idea of encrypted mempools. You hide the content of the transactions all the way down until the order of these transactions in the block is actually committed. So it removes the options for front-running. You cannot observe the intents. And, of course, sandwiching becomes impossible or very risky for searchers. And so the way you encrypt, in a way, is pick your own choice, right? It's about what you want to do with it. So you can have regular encryption, but you can also have, like, some more fancy primitives that allow you to execute or not. In this talk, we'll focus more on the threshold encrypted mempool variant, which is the one that has received the most attention, as far as we're aware, presumably because of, like, its native compatibility with how the validator set is structured. So let's have a quick look at what the academic literature gave us. And I want to first give credit to Shutter, who, as far as I know, is, like, one of the first production deployments to offer a threshold encrypted mempool. There is one notion we need to be aware of in these deployments. It's the idea that, at least in the initial setting, you had, like, this committee of keepers that were controlling the decryption keys, right? And these keys were released based on a certain epoch. So this construction has been studied in this Usenix security paper from 24. And, you know, a few vulnerabilities were identified, but the main one we want to keep in mind is this notion of, like, epochs leaking transactions. So if you decrypt all transactions based on a given epoch, whether or not the transaction is included in the block, it will be revealed. So if, unfortunately, your transaction was not revealed, was not included, it is still revealed at the end of the epoch, which is not what you want at the end of the day. So to address this issue, we moved to, like, a new proposal, which is called batched threshold encryption. And here the idea is to say, we want to be able to select a batch of transactions to decrypt in order to amortize the cost. The other naive option would have been to try and, like, decrypt every single transaction separately, but you pay in terms of communications if you do that. So it seems that it's a good compromise to go for a batch, a decryption of a batch, to reduce the communication that you need to have. So we solve, by doing that, this notion of, like, pending transaction privacy, which is great. And we have the guarantee that, you know, transactions cannot be decrypted when they don't need to. The only issue that was remaining with the batch was that you needed to run a new DKG setup on every epoch. So one proposal called BeatMev, which came slightly afterwards, had this idea of, like, having a single one-time setup, which is even better in a way. Now the question is, like, what's the catch? Why do we stop here or not? Well, we don't stop here because even with one single DKG, you still have a lot of communications to do when you do the setup in the first place, right? And it scales with the amount of parties that you have to run. But more importantly, if you want to rotate committees, so those who hold the shares, that becomes really complex. If you imagine that these committees are mapped, for example, on the validator set or a subset of the validator set, this is a very asynchronous network, right? People come online and offline. So it becomes very tricky to map that to the Ethereum set. And so a new primitive emerged, which is called silent threshold encryption, from a crypto paper in 24. And the idea here was to let the validators generate independently their key pairs and just publish a hint that would allow everyone to then non-interactively combine these hints to produce a joint public key. It is based on a flavor, a weak flavor of witness encryption. It's not very important here, but this is a very nice construction. As usual in academia, when like two good ideas emerge, what do we do? We put them together. So the next paper, and I think what is the one that is probably like state of the art for this construction, is called BeastMev with an S this time. And it's basically unifying the two things we've seen that we found appealing in terms of properties. So combining together batch and silence to have this epochless batch decryption from BeatMev and the silence setup from the crypto 24 paper. The only caveat here is that it's varying based, so it's not post-quantum. And as you're all aware, we probably want post-quantum these days. There is like a follow-up to that from a different team, from both the EF and Flashbots that do propose a post-quantum version, but we're not really covering here. I'm happy to share the slides afterwards. In terms of what the community has to say, right? Now we know kind of like what is the state of the art from an academic standpoint. It's interesting to see how it could be adopted for the protocol.
