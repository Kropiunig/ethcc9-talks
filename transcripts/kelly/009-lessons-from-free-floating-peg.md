A PID stands for Proportional, Integral and Derivative Mechanism. It dates to the 1900s and it's a simple mechanic mechanism to stabilize a measurement into their target. So if you want, let's say for example, if you want to heat water up to 60 degrees, what you do is you measure the gap that you have and you start applying energy to the system depending on that gap. That's the proportional term. The thing with the proportional term is that it tends to overshoot and undershoot. It never stabilizes in the target. So in order to avoid overshooting, what we do is we take the derivative of this curve and we start applying a correction beforehand. So since we know we are reaching the target, we start reducing the energy that you are putting and we stabilize this mechanism without overshooting. The problem is that sometimes we stabilize the mechanism in a position that it's not our target. So this is where we include the integral term, that not only we measure the gap, how far we are from the target, we start measuring how long we've been this far.
