The first problem is time. If you want trustless movement, you have to wait. In optimistic systems, that can mean days. So if you compare the main rollup models, I think the story is actually quite straightforward. Optimistic rollups were a pragmatic response to a world where proving was still too expensive and too immature. At that point, it made sense to assume correctness first and allow the speeds later. You sequence, you post, and then you prove later. And although the system is stronger cryptographically, the proof still arrives after the fact. And because that proving step is delayed, the composability remains broken. So what Surge does differently is it collapses the sequencing steps into one step. And this is the architectural shift. On the left is the status quo where the sequencer builds the blocks, posts the data to L1, and then the system waits, right? Later, the prover picks up that block, generates the proof, and posts the proof to the L1 rollup contract. So there are multiple waiting points in the pipeline. And on the right is the Surge flow. The sequencer builds the block and immediately requests the proof of the state transition. And once the proof is available, the block and the proof are submitted on the L1 together as a single transaction. That changes the properties of the system in a meaningful way. First, it's trustless because the block is ZK proven right before it lands on the L1. Second, it's atomic because the execution path is all or nothing. And third, it's fast because we have compressed what used to be a delayed multi-step settlement flow into a single event. That's not just nicer design technically, it also matters operationally and commercially because it reduces reliance on extra infrastructure that exists mainly to compensate for delay.
