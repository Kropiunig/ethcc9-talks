So the first thing that I want to make clear is that deep block-level customization is already happening. So custom chains, custom rollups are here today. They're not a future-facing thing. They already exist. And what type of customization is this? It can really be broken down into two categories. The first category is around transaction inclusion and ordering. So who is able to participate in a chain, who can send transactions to a chain, and once they've sent those transactions, what order are those transactions placed in? So that's the first level of customization. And you see that in, for example, World, which has built out a rollup where they place humans in blocks ahead of bots. So the idea is that humans have an edge on the bots. Humans can get their transactions in first. Humans are less vulnerable to front-running and other attacks. So that's one spin on it. Another spin on it is what Base is doing. So Base launched an integration with FlashBots called Flash Blocks. And with Flash Blocks, what they effectively do is they build these mini blocks within a broader block, and then these mini blocks get ordered in proper order. So instead of having one big block, you have a bunch of little blocks that you can see in real time, and that changes transaction inclusion and ordering because you get ordered based on the smaller block you're in, not based on the large single block that Base produces for optimism and Ethereum mainnet. The other type of customization is customization of transaction types. So you're seeing custom transaction data being included. So Tempo, for example, is chain-made for stablecoins. They enshrine stablecoins into the execution client layer, so they enshrine stablecoins not on smart contracts, but into the actual nodes themselves. And they have things like a custom memo field for denoting stablecoins and why you've made a certain transfer. Hyperliquid, of course, is also a chain where they are essentially a completely custom chain from the ground up for perps. And if you look at these examples, like we're talking about Base, we're talking about Hyperliquid, we're talking about World, we're talking about Tempo, this is the vast majority of usage in crypto today, is happening on custom chains. People aren't framing it that way. People aren't talking about it that way, but it is already some of the most adopted chains in the space. So we are really seeing an unprecedented rise in this adoption. But this didn't come out of nowhere. What I do want to talk about is how we got here. Why did these custom chains only emerge now? Why didn't they emerge 5 to 10 years ago? And where do we think it will be going forward? So that's one of the key questions, is why now? Why didn't people do this earlier? And the answer is, if you look at the history, the feasibility of this was actually very recent. So from all the way from Bitcoin's launch in 2009, all the way to around 2017, everyone was actually self-hosting their own nodes. So when you booted up a Bitcoin wallet, this is an example of a screenshot of a node from 2011. You would have to sync your node. So your wallet would be syncing. And this sync would take hours, days, now even weeks. So just to use the chain, you think that having to save your seed phrase is bad. Imagine having to launch your software, sync tens of gigabytes, wait hours or days, and then you can finally transact. This could sometimes take a long time. Here's one screenshot I pulled where it hits 18 hours left to sync, and they're at 99% for a Bitcoin node. So every user who wanted to transact had to do this. And this was true for Ethereum too. One of the very first Ethereum wallets was a wallet known as Mist. And every time you launched it, you'd have to sync all the blocks. And you'd get a user experience after syncing that looks kind of like this. You could directly input all your smart contract data to make transactions. It was not the best user experience in the world. We have improved a lot since then. But the key thing to understand here is that in the early days, wallets were nodes. They were one and the same. And that meant that customization would require upgrades to every wallet app. And you really, really did not want to trigger resyncs because every single user, if you broke syncing or you made some kind of breaking change, would have to then resync over hours, days, or weeks. So the iteration cycle was very, very slow. Imagine every time you relaunch your wallet, you have to wait hours before it's usable again. That's what things were like only, you know, under 10 years ago. And the thing that we saw was that this shifted. So 2017 to 2021 is broadly what I'd call the mempool era. So Ethereum had increased a lot in usage. You can see 2017 is right around where that giant spike is on the chart. And after that point, everyone running their own nodes was not really viable. Syncs moved from hours to days to weeks. And you couldn't expect every single user to wait for that process to complete before they could do anything on chain. So you saw the emergence of professional RPC providers in 2017 and 2018, like Infura, Alchemy, and QuickNode. The idea that you could install a browser extension or download a mobile app and start using it immediately and not hours later was actually quite novel. And the professionalization of node hosting was also novel. It meant that instead of having to coordinate upgrades with every single user's wallet software, you would coordinate upgrades to nodes with a small number of professional node providers. And the network still had transparency and still had some level of verifiability to it because if you sent a transaction from your wallet, it would be pending in the mempool. So you could go inspect the state of what nodes had. You could see that your transaction was there, and you wouldn't know when it would be processed. You might be waiting a while, but you would know that it was in this pending state. From there, the vast majority of transactions through 2021 were visible. So you could inspect it yourself. Because in proof-of-work, everyone is racing to basically find the same data, find the best way to essentially find the corresponding hash for a block. Everyone's racing for this at the same time, and you don't know who's going to win. So you wouldn't know who's next, which made these MEV supply chains relatively difficult. You wouldn't know who to send your transactions to because you didn't know who would have the next block. But with proof-of-stake, validators are actually known, 32.
