My talk is about ADL and what happened October 10th and some of the things that we're doing to basically protect traders from wrongfully being liquidated in ADL. So just a quick review. Liquidation versus ADL. So in a liquidation, what's happening is the loser is being removed from the system and replaced with a different trader. So usually that other trader is also in the order book and they're matched against the winner. What happens here is other traders are not affected other than the loser being removed. In an ADL situation, there's no other resting orders on the book, so you can't replace the losers, so the winning side is also removed. So both sides are removed from the system. This is a pretty drastic measure, and the reason why you have to do this is if you don't ADL people out, the protocol becomes insolvent, and at that point you're basically unable to use the exchange. So the invariants that the protocol is built on, i.e. like winners and losers get paid out, that invariant doesn't get held. So it's a very important reason why ADL exists. So a big part of liquidation mechanism is like how perp dexes do margin. The simplest one is isolated margin, where every winner and trader brings their margin, and then the most common one that you see is cross margin, where you're able to share your profits and losses across different markets. So for example, if you're long Bitcoin and short ETH, and your long is up 50K and your short is down, let's say, 25K, the protocol nets the profits and losses out, so you're not forced to close out the losing position, and you can hold it longer. Traders do this for multiple reasons, for collecting funding, for doing some sort of spread trade. A lot of reasons are there for these types of mechanisms. The thing with isolated margin is, in that situation I described, where you have a profit in Bitcoin and a loss in ETH, you would be forced to close out some of the profits in Bitcoin to hold your ETH position. So you would take some profits, move the P&L over to your ETH position to make sure both positions stay open. Now, this all seems fine, especially cross margin. It's a nice way for people to keep positions open, but the issue comes up when there are these market crashes, and October 10th is a great example. So people are putting on spread trades or funding trades where they're long Bitcoin, short ETH. But what happens is, when the short ETH position, which is basically in profit when the market's crashing, ADLs out because the counterparty needs to be replaced, but there's no counterparty to that. So the solution that we're proposing is called hybrid margin. What we're trying to do is get the best of both worlds. So again, to recap, isolated margin, you can't move your unrealized P&L across markets. And in cross margin, unrealized P&L is automatically netted, but that also causes cross contagion risk. Hybrid margin is a system where you as a trader are able to move unrealized P&L to the different markets to protect your positions.
