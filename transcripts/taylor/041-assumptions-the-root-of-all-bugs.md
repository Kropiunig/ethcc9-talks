# Assumptions: The Root of All Bugs
**OxFlint_** (Certora) --- Taylor Stage

Okay, yes, well, good morning, everyone, and welcome to my presentation on assumptions, the root of all bugs. Now, I am, well, Flint, senior security researcher at Cetora, and my job is to hunt bugs, to look at protocols and see for flaws in the code. And whenever I present findings to my clients, I always see frustration on the faces of developers. Always like the question internally, why do we have so many bugs? I tried so hard to write secure code, and there's still this flaw and this flaw, and thank God the auditor was there, because otherwise we would have gotten wrecked. And I thought for a long time, like, why is this? These are developers that are so good, but they still make so many errors. And I think that the answer lies in assumptions. Basically, the gap between assumptions and realities. That's where bugs are born. But what are assumptions? Assumptions are basically everything that we assume to be true, but haven't verified. They're a basic feature of human cognition. I don't know that this floor can bear my weight, but I assume it can, because otherwise I can't really function. It's what Kahneman calls, what you see is all there is. The mind takes limited information, makes a best guess, and presents it as established fact. That's what we do every day, hundreds and thousands of times. We need to in order to function. But in the world of DeFi, where immutable code carries billions of dollars, the slightest deviation between an assumption and reality can have catastrophic events. These assumptions exist at every level of complexity, from the cross-chain, cross-domain, to composability, function, logic, and the machine level. For cross-domain assumptions, I can give an example of a recent staking protocol where it was the auditor. The protocol had a unique feature of migration, where an off-chain system captured the entire state of the on-chain system through events and held it. And whenever a migration was necessary, a script queried the off-chain protocol, submitted the entire state, and redeployed with the exact complete state as before. Very unique system, and the components in isolation worked really well. There were audits, Web2 and Web3, which confirmed this. But I know that these teams often work in isolation. So I asked myself, what assumptions does each team have on the other, which need to be true in order for the system to work? The Web2 developers need to assume that the events capture the total state of the system. And the Web3 developers need to assume that the Web2 system correctly submits the entire state, which was correct in both cases. But when I went deeper on the off-chain protocol, I saw on the actual SQL query that it filtered on staked events, which is a logical assumption. In most staking systems, you deposit, you get staked, and the staked event is emitted. So, and anything that's staked, that contains the entire stake. But here, in this system, you could deposit and then not stake, because there are multiple choices for staking pools. So if the migration had gone ahead, then a small percentage of users, a few thousand, a few million euros or dollars, would have simply been assumed out of existence by the simple deviation between the assumption and the reality. On the composability assumptions, in every ecosystem, may that be Ethereum, for example, thousands of protocols interact with each other, integrate with each other, and they make assumptions on the other's functioning, which are very often correct and sometimes not. In the case of Abracadabra and GMX2, this was such a case. Because normally, a deposit in 99% of all systems, a deposit is final, it is atomic. Once you have deposited an event, it means, yeah, somebody deposited. And Abracadabra assumed this was true for GMX and allowed people to take loans with collateral of GMX as, well, deposits at GMX as collateral. But GMX had a unique feature where deposits are only pending and they need to be executed by a keeper. An attacker saw the flaw and engineered deposits, which could get rejected in GMX. But before they were rejected, he borrowed 13 million in Abracadabra and walked away. Every system alone was correct, but the interaction was based on a simple flaw. Function levels, we can have the example of Euler, the famous hack of don't need to reserves, where a very small, insignificant function was added so that users could clear their accounts from some remaining dust. It was so small that the basic health check to ensure that an action doesn't make your position insolvent was not added because it's harmless. I mean, it's only dust and who on earth would destroy their own collateral? So the assumption was, this is insignificant. This is not important. But an attacker saw something else and he managed to destroy so much of his collateral that his position became deeply insolvent and through self-liquidation with a discount, he managed to steal 300 million from Euler. Now, most of the assumptions we've seen are kind of complex or delicate or require very in-depth knowledge and logic. But that's not always the case. In a recent audit, I had a developer who had a decimal check smaller than six and my recommendation was, well, you need to extend this to say smaller than six and smaller than 18 so that the decimals are bound on both sides. And as you can see, this was implemented a bit too literal because this cannot happen. So the checks become useless. And when I told him, he was, well, frustrated, but also embarrassed because he said, look, I watched this code like dozens of times. I was working on it. It's like, how did I not see this? But when we write code, we write so much code that we don't reread every line every time. We can't. For efficiency reasons, your mind says, okay, I know I wrote this. You scan it and you continue. The mind, the eye sees the intention, but the compiler sees the instruction. And lastly, on machine level assumptions, we have strange and storage where a developer assumed that a consumed value within the same transaction could be safely repurposed without clearing it. The EVM made no such promise and an attacker exploited this. So we have assumptions, problems on every level of conception with devastating effects. So what can we do about this? Well, the first response is always testing because we need tests to find bugs, test to confirm intended working, more tests, and we find more bugs. But our tools, our tests inherit our assumptions because what tests does the developer normally write? Well, one, the happy path tests. My deposit actually deposits. My withdrawal actually withdraws. And then a number of tests for the list of edge cases that he has in his head, like, look, this can go wrong, or I remember this hack. Let's make sure X, Y, and Z. But why would he write a test on an incorrect assumption which he is not conscious of? It makes no sense. If he was conscious of the incorrect assumption, he'd simply fix the code. So tests can help in the sense that if you have an incorrect assumption which falls within all those edge cases, it will find it, but that only covers a small part. Next week, we have formal verification, which is unique because it checks state across, well, checks properties across all possible states, so it does not take into account your assumptions or your subjective nature. But at the same time, formal engineers are also humans. They also have assumptions. They need to make assumptions when they look at the design of the protocol. How is it intended to work? What features are enabled and will be enabled? What are reasonable bounds on the inputs, on the configuration parameters set by admins? And if this is incorrect, then you have a problem. So formal verification helps really well, but the scope changes from my code is correct to my specification is complete, which in itself is an assumption. So if tests can only partially help, perhaps we just need more experience. Yes, this does work. We all know that an experienced senior developer writes less bugs than a junior one. But at the same time, this is a long, costly, and painful process because every lesson learned means a bug missed, either found by an auditor, paid out in a bounty protocol, or you wake up to see that your protocol got wrecked and X amount of funds were stolen. So it does work, but it is the long and painful process. So what else can we do? I think we can recommend a practice to help developers surface these assumptions, something which I call the inward audit. Now, this is not a magic pill or some AI-powered solution that fixes the world because the problem lies within the human mind, assumptions. So the only thing we can do is look inward and examine ourselves. This means, basically, that you pause and you say, what do I assume to be true when I'm writing this code? What do I know to be true? It means, practically...


Asking at several times or continuously, what am I assuming about the architecture? Do I know or do I assume the integrating protocol's behavior? What is this function's actual potential influence on the protocol? Am I actually writing what I think that I'm writing, or am I making a blunder? Am I assuming what about the execution environment? And finally, above all, what must hold true for this system not to fail? Now, as I've said, this is not a magic pill. This is something, a practice, which takes time and effort. But on the other hand, what is the alternative? Because probably, yes, the sun will rise tomorrow. The view function and the library function work probably. The callback hook probably is harmless. And the auditor will most likely find all the bugs. I'll do my best. But probably is not a good security model. E, suppose to say thank you. There we go. OK, so I think we have some time for questions, if there are any. And enjoy your day.
