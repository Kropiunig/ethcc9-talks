# The Ethereum Kernel
**Martin Hansen** (Ethereum Foundation) --- Taylor Stage

Okay, I think we're ready to go. Great to see so many people here. I'm Martin Hansen. I work at the funding coordination team at the Ethereum Foundation and today I'm going to tell you about the Ethereum kernel. It's a project that I've been working on for a couple of months and it's a work stream at our funding coordination team. Before we get started too much, I just want to tell you a little bit about our team and what we're working on. Funding coordination at the EF, we have three broad high-level goals. We want to increase net new public goods funding into the ecosystem. We want to more effectively match funder supply and demand more efficiently. And lastly, we want to reduce ecosystem demand on EF for funding. So that's part of why this work stream came about and you will hear a little bit more how the kernel work stream references some of these broader goals that we have. Now at a high level, what is the kernel? Let's get that defined first. It's a bit of a mouthful, but it's essentially the critical functions that ensures the world computer's uptime, programmability, and accessibility. We'll be getting much more into the specifics of what that means, but at a high level, you can think about the components that make sure that the world computer is running and it's usable. We set out to map out the Ethereum kernel for a couple of reasons. One was that we think there's a huge need for more diversified funding sources for critical infrastructure that our whole ecosystem depends on. And when you couple that with finite runways of many of these funders, whether it's DAOs, whether it's non-profit organizations like the EF, and we have this thousand-year horizon of building civilizational infrastructure for hundreds of years, there's a bit of a mismatch. And as we think about these diversified funding sources, we also need to think about where it goes. And so allocation is really, really important. And our perspective here is that as we think about allocation, we need to prioritize the most critical functions first because they sort of make out the base layer. And related to that, we believe that there are really strong benefits to neutral funding. And when I say neutral funding, it can be public goods funding, but it can also be value flows that go directly to dependencies and so on. One of the benefits is obviously that there are no strings attached the same way that we see with other types of funding sources. So you won't have to make the same kind of trade-offs. Another thing is that it can protect you against more abrupt shutdowns. So if you're part of a larger organization and they reprioritize, they restructure, suddenly projects are shut down, and it could be quite critical if that's an important component in the world computer. And lastly, it can enable you to think long-term. You're not optimizing for the short term. You're not trying to meet some target for the next quarter. You can think years ahead and try to do what's right today for a five-year horizon, let's say. So that's why we advocate quite a lot for neutral funding sources. Now, we're going to talk a little bit more about Ethereum at a high level, and then we'll get into the kernel more specifically. The reason why the kernel looks different and a certain way for Ethereum is because we have high expectations of Ethereum. There's certain properties we want to see out of Ethereum. And as such, it's going to look very different what the Ethereum kernel is versus some centralized database, let's say. One thing that we expect out of Ethereum is 100% network uptime. That requires quite a bit to achieve that. Another thing is some of the properties of Ethereum, sort of being censorship resistant, being open source, having privacy, being secure. These are properties that we all need to optimize for, and we can always improve on those. And then lastly, a lot of people in the Ethereum ecosystem have been talking about passing the walkaway test, being able to one day step away from Ethereum and it just running and being reliable and secure for people to use. And these are not easy things to build. And therefore, the kernel is going to look quite different than what we might otherwise see in other ecosystems. Now, how do we get here? How do we set up to map this? What was our methodology, so to say? The key thing was that we talked to a lot of technical domain experts, particularly from the Ethereum Foundation's protocol cluster across different domains. We tried to get their take on what the kernel is to them. And we tried to synthesize that and sort of give our take on it. And on top of that, we did a ton of deep research on different elements of that, try to go down multiple different rabbit holes. And related to that, we tried to study the evolution of critical Ethereum infrastructure and see how it has changed over time and whether there are some patterns that we can connect and what that might tell you about the future. And I think this was one interesting sort of learning point that the kernel has definitely changed a lot over time during Ethereum's history. We would argue that it was much smaller earlier on. There were fewer clients. When you look at the, there were fewer hard forks, but also hard forks with less sort of breaking changes. Many clients were working for years after they actually, after they stopped maintaining them. And that has changed quite a lot till today. Today, we have a lot of clients. We have a more accelerated protocol development environment. We have a lot of big features that have landed on Ethereum. And we need far more coordination. So you see much more sort of governance coordination work, but you also see far more testing work. You have way more researchers than we used to. And that puts us in a good place in that we can accelerate the roadmap and we can move faster. Now the big question is, of course, what is the kernel going to look like in the future? And what timeline do you put on that? There's a lot of research into some of these endgame solutions, whether it's lean Ethereum or it's the straw map, it's CK EVM, or it's making Ethereum post-quantum secure. And this could potentially lead to a reduced kernel in the future because certain parts of the protocol might harden. But obviously that is a question. And we will have to see how that changes over time. The kernel is the core and it's also a much smaller part of a much bigger ecosystem. It sits at the core. It forms these critical functions. But there's a lot going around it, of course. There's obviously other tooling that during this research didn't prove to necessarily be absolutely critical for the uptime of Ethereum, but still really important and useful for users and for developers. And then, of course, there's the whole application layer where a lot of exciting things happen and things are growing really, really nicely. The reason why we said we sort of highlight neutral funding so much in the beginning here and why that our talks generally center a lot about the importance of neutral funding is that when you look at the at the kernel itself at the center, it's really, really important that you have neutrality. You have less trade-offs being made and less sort of gateways at the very base. Whereas the further you go out, the more funding opportunities are present to you and you won't necessarily have to make the same kind of trade-offs that you would have at the core. So I think that that's why we keep highlighting neutral funding for the kernel as being really important. What we're going to do now is we're going to build the Ethereum kernel one component at a time together. And we start with the heart of the kernel, the largest components, which is the clients, execution layer and consensus layer clients. In all my talks with various different domain experts, it's become quite clear to me that at this point, client diversity has become quite synonymous with Ethereum and not having client diversity, essentially, it's not really the same Ethereum that we know of today. So we do need to have some client diversity. And of course, the properties of client diversity, again, is fairly synonymous with Ethereum. It makes our network far more resilient and it has so far secured unmatched uptime and no downtime. The next thing we have is with client diversity, we do need coordination. We need these clients to work and develop in tandem and be in sync. So very important to have some individuals working on protocol coordination and relatedly also governing this whole upgrade processes and documenting how things are changing, possibly even more so in the age of AI. Then we have research as well. Research and prototyping generally falls outside of the client teams and that has certain benefits. Here, I think it's important to distinguish the importance, distinguish the difference between short-term research and long-term research.
